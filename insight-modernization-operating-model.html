A Five-Phase Operating Model for Agentic Modernization

Bounded migration work needs different discipline than continuous delivery: five sequential stages, two iteration loops, and named human owners at every gate.

Insights · Agentic legacy modernization

When enterprises plan an agentic modernization program, most reach for the operating discipline they already run: sprint cadences, continuous backlogs, teams sized for years of ongoing delivery. That discipline is built for live applications with evolving scope. Modernization is a different shape of work. The scope is bounded, the target is well-defined, and the engagement runs quarters rather than years. It needs its own operating model: stage-sequenced rather than sprint-cadenced, wrapping human governance around the agent fleet that does the migration work.

Same principles, different shape

The modernization operating model shares its principles with the continuous-delivery model it sits beside: agents constrained by structure, humans validating rather than implementing every step, a named owner for each artifact. The two differ where the use case forces them to. Continuous delivery runs two staggered sprint cycles indefinitely; modernization runs five sequential stages once. Continuous delivery iterates per change request; modernization runs a per-module migration-validation loop until four validation gates pass. In continuous delivery a developer sits in the loop on every change; in modernization a Modernization Expert validates each iteration's target code output and a Product Owner validates the parity contract at UAT. One engagement lasts years, the other quarters. A client that needs both uses the modernization model for the bounded migration, then transitions to continuous delivery once the modernized system is live.

The five phases

The first two phases build the modernization ontologies. The next two execute and validate the migration. The fifth maintains the system through hand-over.

  • Discovery and Analysis (two to four weeks). The client's CTO or VP Engineering sponsors; the team fixes the modernization scope at module level, confirms the target stack, and picks the MVP module that will pilot the migration. Ingestion and enrichment agents run across the legacy estate to produce a preliminary Source-state ontology, feeding a full analysis report and a detailed migration plan with target architecture.
  • Ontology Generation and Enrichment (one to three weeks). The team decides the aperture: which legacy modules get decomposed at full statement depth, and which, typically those slated for retirement, are captured at summary depth. Output is the complete Source-state ontology in a graph database, a Target-state ontology from the blueprint, and human-readable extractions: a business requirements document, BDD function tests, end-to-end test scripts, and a code chatbot.
  • MVP Migration (six to twelve weeks). One module, migrated end to end. The Product Owner and Architect annotate every Source-state node as Retain, Modify, Replace, or Retire. Migration agents produce target code candidates; four validation gates check each one; the loop repeats until the gates pass. This phase also sets the autonomy level the agents will operate at and produces a documented per-module workflow.
  • Scaled Migration (quarters, paced by release cadence). The MVP workflow runs at scale across the remaining modules, ordered by the dependency map and risk classification from discovery, with agents operating at the engagement-tuned steady-state autonomy level.
  • UAT, Deployment, and Maintenance (per release wave, then through hand-over). The Product Owner and the client's UAT team accept each module; deployment engineering ships module groups and monitors them. Outputs include the deployed modern system, technical documentation, and a knowledge graph in a state fit for ongoing operation.

Two loops, two speeds

Phases three and four run two iteration loops in parallel, and they are different in kind. The migration-validation loop is automated and fast: per code candidate, typically minutes to hours, regenerating output until the four gates pass. The SME tuning loop is human-driven and slow: per module, typically weeks, where review of gate-passed output drives adjustments to agent prompts, configurations, and annotations.

The tuning loop is what makes the model accumulate engagement-specific knowledge. By the end of the MVP phase, the agents operate at the autonomy level the engagement supports. By the end of scaled migration, the per-module workflow is calibrated and the SME's annotation patterns are captured in configuration, which is why the later modules move faster than the first.

Validation, not per-step approval

The Modernization Expert is the named human owner of the agents across the project, paired with the engagement's Chief Architect on validation and with the Product Owner on the parity contract. The Expert reviews at three defined points: whenever an iteration passes all four gates (is the output consistent with the annotations and the target blueprint, and is the gate evidence sufficient to advance the module), at the end of each module migration (is it deployment-ready, has Product Owner and Architect feedback landed in the configuration), and at hand-over (is the modern system operating as expected, can the knowledge graph transfer).

This pattern is what makes agent autonomy credible to a skeptical buyer. The agents run autonomously through migration and validation; the Expert reviews bounded outputs at defined gates instead of approving each agent action. The team stays small because of it. A 500K-line modernization typically engages one Modernization Expert, one Chief Architect, two to three Product Owners, and one Architect. A 3M-line estate runs on two Experts, one Chief Architect with a supporting senior architect, four to six Product Owners, and two Architects.

The hand-over decides what comes next

When modernization completes, the knowledge graph is fully populated against the modern stack, and the client has three paths. Take over operationally: the ontology transfers to the client's internal team and the engagement ends. Continue into a continuous SDLC engagement: the Source-state ontology seeds the four-layer Code Ontology and the same custodians carry on. Or retire the engagement entirely, with the graph remaining the client's property. The paths combine; a common shape is modernization delivered, a continuous SDLC engagement for a defined initial period, typically eighteen months, then client takeover with a residual support agreement.
