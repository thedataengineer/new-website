What Spec-Driven Delivery Looks Like in Practice

Three anonymized engagements: where spec-driven development pays off, where it hits its ceiling, and what raises it.

Continuous SDLCSemantic EngineeringThree case archetypes

Spec-driven development is the current default for serious AI-assisted engineering: write the specification, gate the sprint on it, generate code against it. It works, and then it stops scaling. We have run the same Semantic Engineering methodology across continuous-delivery engagements that differ on every meaningful axis, and the pattern is consistent enough to name. Here are three of them: one past the complexity threshold, one approaching it, and one living inside it.

One methodology, three entry points

The three teams share nothing on the surface. A global content research and product company with over two million lines of production code. A utility management and billing platform with a clean greenfield UI workstream. An automotive aftermarket platform suite running the most operationally mature spec-driven practice in our portfolio. Different industries, different stacks, different starting contexts.

What they share is the failure mode. Once the codebase, the design system, or the team count crosses a complexity threshold, the spec alone can no longer govern the work. The trigger differs by context; the structural response is the same: introduce machine-readable structure at the layer where the spec is failing, and let the AI agents consume that structure as context.

Brownfield: two million lines, no global context

The content company had already tried AI coding tools. Individual engineers got value in pockets, typically isolated UI prototypes. At the global level, where changes had to coordinate across repositories and respect contracts owned by other teams, the agent produced little of substance. The missing ingredient was global context, and no amount of prompting supplies it.

We built a four-layer knowledge graph of the full 2.25 million line codebase, covering code, architecture, functional behavior, and design, in two to three weeks. The first demo answered a deliberately open-ended client question: what would it take to replace faceted search with semantic search? The Impact Analysis Agent returned a ten-page report covering functional changes, affected architectural entities, UI components, code modules, and database schema impact, from the question alone, with no specification authored.

The operating change followed. Brownfield impact analysis that used to cost a senior engineer three to five days of archaeology now runs in eight minutes against the live graph. Cross-team conflicts are caught at a PR validation gate instead of in meetings. BDD test scenarios are generated from the functional layer of the graph: 93.4% test coverage with zero manual authoring.

One detail matters for anyone evaluating this approach: the extracted graph is not trusted on faith. An integrity assessment ran against all four layers. Two passed cleanly; two surfaced structural defects that would have made agent output unreliable. The defects went into a remediation backlog, and all four layers passed by the end of the second month. Without that gate, the defects stay invisible until production incidents start accumulating.

Greenfield: discipline that outgrew itself

The utility billing platform is the more common story. Its new UI workstream began clean: React, TypeScript, a Figma-driven design workflow, and spec-driven discipline that held for the first several sprints. Then growth changed the math. The component library expanded, and AI-generated components began duplicating parts the spec never referenced. Multiple workstreams converged, adding coordination no single spec could capture. Designers shipped Figma screens faster than developers could check them for component reuse.

The team did not deploy the full methodology. It adopted one slice, the design layer: the component library extracted into a structured ontology, connected to the Figma wireframes, and consulted by the model before any new component is generated. The first governed sprint returned 53% component reuse from the existing design system, 23 generated files with zero errors and zero boundary violations, better than 95% Figma-to-code accuracy on first attempt, and per-component build time down 85 to 90%, from 15 to 31 hours manual to 1 to 2 hours. The overall workstream timeline improved 30 to 40% with approval workflows unchanged.

The lesson for buyers: adoption stages by layer. Start with the one that matches your active bottleneck, take the measurable win, and expand as complexity demands.

Mature spec-driven delivery: the ceiling from the inside

The third engagement shows spec-driven delivery executed about as well as it can be. Facing an acquired product with over a million lines of unmaintainable code and acute revenue pressure, the team rewrote it in five to six months, with more than twenty engineers and QA in feature pods, a written agent constitution, supervisor skills to keep the agent deterministic, and a custom tooling plugin. The rewrite succeeded. The ceiling arrived anyway.

  • Pull requests run 300 to 400 files and roughly 30,000 lines; each review cycle takes two-plus weeks.
  • Sixteen to eighteen hour days are routine during feature-completion windows.
  • The team finds the bottom 30 to 40 percent of engineers cannot operate inside the pattern.
  • As pod count grows, no reviewer can hold cross-feature coherence in mind, because a spec is a document, not a structure you can query across teams.

The engagement lead put it plainly: code generation is the easy part of the problem; the hard part is managing QA and governance. That is the ceiling in one sentence, and it is the load the four-layer graph is built to carry: structural validation of generated changes before human review, test scenarios generated from the functional layer instead of manual spec QA, and a queryable substrate for cross-team governance.

What extraction also finds

A caution from an adjacent engagement, a social media marketing platform. Its backend had been split into products along organizational lines instead of technical service boundaries, and four to five years of evolution produced duplication no team owned and no architect could fully see. When we extracted the functional layer, the duplicates surfaced immediately: the same business outcome appeared in multiple product graphs with different implementation paths. The extraction output was the refactor roadmap the team had been deferring. The graph is context for agents and a diagnostic surface for leadership at the same time.

Three entry points, one destination. If your systems are past the threshold, extract the full graph up front; the brownfield extraction capability makes that weeks of work, not months. If you are approaching it, start with the layer under the most pressure. If you are living inside the spec-driven ceiling, the graph is what raises it.
