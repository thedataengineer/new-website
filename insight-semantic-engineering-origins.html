Where Semantic Engineering Came From

Two investments, five years apart, converged into the methodology that now runs our AI engagements. Here is the story, with dates.

Methodology · Origin story2017 → 2025

Semantic Engineering came from the convergence of two long-running investments inside Accion Labs. One was organizational: a 2017 framework that codified what good product owners, architects, and UX designers must produce. The other was technical: a 2022 discovery that knowledge graphs could stop a transformer model from hallucinating. Each ran independently for years. Together they became a methodology that was proven in client production long before it had a name.

A 2017 framework called Breeze

In 2017, Accion Labs was scaling fast and the quality of architects, product owners, and UX designers varied across engagements. Engineering leadership needed a standard newer hires could be trained against, so it built one. The framework was called Breeze, and it defined, for each role, the minimum set of artifacts and decisions a practitioner had to produce. Product owners owed persona definitions, outcome specifications, and scenario walkthroughs. Architects owed service decompositions, entity definitions, and integration patterns. UX designers owed component libraries, journey maps, and quantitative user metrics that went beyond satisfaction surveys.

Breeze worked. It was also operationally heavy. Keeping the artifacts current across hundreds of engagements demanded continuous coordination. Newer practitioners produced compliant artifacts; senior practitioners often skipped them and ran on tacit understanding. Under deadline pressure the framework was always at risk of being abandoned. Breeze proved what minimal governance looks like for each engineering role. What it lacked was a mechanism to sustain that governance at scale.

2022: a model that would not stop inventing

Five years later, a small Accion Labs team started the firm's first GenAI project: a drug discovery application for a pharma leader. Work began in Q1 2022 and reached production in Q3 2022. ChatGPT was still ten months away. The team chose a transformer model because conventional NLP could not handle the language of pharmaceutical research.

During development the model hallucinated badly enough to make the application useless without intervention. Instead of finding plausible pathways within the validated decision architecture the researchers needed, it invented its own. The output was confident and wrong, which is the most expensive kind of wrong.

The fix was structural. The team built a decision tree of the validated pathways, gave the model the tree as constrained options, and asked it to pick rather than invent. The application worked. The decision tree grew into an ontology, the ontology grew into a knowledge graph, and the same constraint pattern held in every other domain the team applied it to. The insight underneath: a large language model paired with a structured knowledge graph produces precise, traceable, auditable output, where the model alone produces confident plausibility. The graph constrains what can be chosen. The model becomes a chooser rather than an inventor.

2023: productized as KAPS, before the name existed

The graph approach went commercial well before the branding did. In 2023 the team productized it as KAPS, the Knowledge and Analytics Platform, and rolled it out across customer engagements: ingest the domain knowledge into a graph, ground the LLM against the graph, and let the structure constrain what the model can produce. By the end of 2023, KAPS was the de-facto methodology across the AI engagements the firm ran. That timeline matters for anyone evaluating the methodology today. It ran in commercial production for two years before it became a brand.

The convergence, 2022 to 2025

The two investments met when AI matured enough to operationalize Breeze's manual governance through agents reading and writing against knowledge graphs. Breeze's per-role structures became the four-layer ontology, rendered in a form machines can reason over. The product-owner artifacts became the Functional Ontology, the architecture artifacts the Architecture Ontology, the UX artifacts the Design Ontology. Code, which Breeze had left out because code is the output of the other three, became the fourth layer once AI could extract code-level structure from the AST.

The agent fleet operating on those four layers is what made the discipline sustainable. The maintenance burden that defeated Breeze's manual blueprint is now handled by the KG Sync Agent. The drift that defeats text documentation is prevented structurally. Senior practitioners stop being the bottleneck because their context-assembly work lives in the system through the Impact Analysis Agent.

The convergence happened gradually. KAPS carried the approach commercially in 2023. Breeze.AI and ASIMOV brought it to the SDLC and modernization shapes of work in 2024. By 2025 the discipline was formal enough to name, and it took the name Semantic Engineering. The platform that operationalizes it took the name of its 2017 ancestor. Breeze.AI is what Breeze became once AI could do the maintenance work humans had been doing by hand.

Why both halves mattered

Neither investment would have produced the methodology on its own. Without the 2022 drug-discovery moment, the knowledge graph never enters the architecture; the firm would have kept trying to scale Breeze's manual blueprint with better tooling, and the blueprint would have kept losing to deadlines. Without Breeze, the knowledge graph would have been a technical capability without a clear application: impressive graphs that did not match the structure of the work practitioners do, used in narrow technical contexts and never extended into the operating model.

The convergence is what makes the methodology durable. The graphs are constrained to capture what practitioners need to govern. The practitioner roles get a structural medium for their work. The agents bridge the two.

Buying a discipline, not an AI strategy

One conclusion from this history disagrees with the common framing of AI-driven methodologies. The underlying discipline, codifying the minimal governance structures for each engineering role, existed before AI and would hold value even if AI did not exist. AI is the enabling technology that made the discipline operationally viable at scale. Adopting Semantic Engineering means adopting an engineering discipline that AI happens to make sustainable, not an AI strategy as such. Clients who grasp that distinction adopt the methodology more durably than clients who treat it as a wrapper around an AI tool.

The methodology keeps moving. Patterns that hold across multiple engagements become canonical; patterns that fail are removed. When industry vocabulary shifts, the methodology adapts to stay interoperable. And the convergence pattern itself, two investments meeting at the right technical moment, is structural and reproducible in domains beyond the SDLC.
